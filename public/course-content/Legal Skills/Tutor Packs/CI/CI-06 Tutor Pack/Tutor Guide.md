**CI-06 | TUTOR MODEL ANSWERS**

### Task 1 — Model Answer

The client is Noah Miles, warehouse supervisor. The matter concerns an employee has been suspended after an argument and alleged safety breach, with conflicting accounts. The interview purpose is to take structured instructions, understand the client’s objectives, test the documentary record, and identify what evidence is missing before any advice or external communication is prepared. The interviewer should treat the client’s account as important but not conclusive. The urgency and emotion in the materials mean the interview must slow the facts down, check dates and documents, and make clear that the student is gathering information for supervisor review rather than giving final legal advice.

### Task 2 — Model Answer

The available documents provide an initial evidence base but not a complete answer. The intake form and initial email identify the client’s objective and the pressure point. The document extracts and message or timeline materials give partial support for the client’s account, but also show gaps or complications. The supervisor instruction confirms the interview should focus on chronology, evidence, objectives, risk, and careful communication. Source-backed facts should be tied to the exhibit that proves them. Assertions, emotional language, and assumptions should be recorded as the client’s account unless independently supported.

### Task 3 — Model Answer

The key missing information is the full underlying agreement or policy documents, complete communications, evidence supporting the timeline, all relevant photographs or records, and confirmation of any external steps already taken. The interviewer should ask for original documents rather than screenshots where possible, full message threads rather than extracts, and dates for all key events. The student should also clarify who else was involved, what has been said to third parties, what outcome the client wants, and whether there are urgent safety, financial, reputational, or procedural risks requiring supervisor escalation.

### Task 4 — Model Answer

Open questions should invite Noah Miles to give a narrative without being pushed towards a conclusion. Suitable questions include: ‘Can you tell me in your own words what has happened from the beginning?’ ‘What was the first thing that made you concerned?’ ‘What outcome are you hoping for from us?’ ‘What documents do you have that we have not yet seen?’ and ‘Has anything been said or sent externally already?’ These questions help build rapport and reveal facts the documents may not show.

### Task 5 — Model Answer

Focused follow-up questions should pin down dates, documents and specific communications. The interviewer should ask when each key event occurred, who was present or involved, what was said, what evidence exists, and what documents are missing. Follow-ups should also test the points that complicate the client’s account, including any missed steps, informal arrangements, disputed messages, or incomplete records. The questions should remain neutral, for example: ‘What happened next?’ or ‘Do you have the full message thread?’ rather than ‘They were clearly at fault, weren’t they?’

### Task 6 — Model Answer

The sensitive issues are those likely to make the client defensive or distressed, including any possible contribution to the problem, missing documents, inconsistent dates, emotional pressure, or risky communications. The interviewer should use careful wording: ‘I need to ask about this because the other side may rely on it,’ or ‘This does not mean you have done anything wrong, but we need to understand the full picture.’ The interviewer should acknowledge the client’s concern while making clear that the firm must check all evidence before reaching a view.

### Task 7 — Model Answer

The interviewer must not promise an outcome, confirm liability, accuse another party, recommend sending aggressive wording, or advise on final legal strategy during the fact-gathering interview. The interviewer may explain that the purpose is to understand the facts, identify documents, and prepare information for the supervising solicitor. They should avoid jurisdiction-specific legal conclusions unless instructed and should escalate urgent safety, confidentiality, reputational, financial, or procedural points to the supervisor before any external action is taken.

### Task 8 — Model Answer

A suitable opening would be: ‘Thank you for speaking with me. My role today is to understand what has happened, what outcome you are hoping for, and what documents we need to review. I will ask some open questions first, then more focused questions about dates and documents. I will not give final advice today, but I will summarise what I understand and flag next steps for the supervising solicitor.’ This sets expectations and reduces the risk of premature reliance.

### Task 9 — Model Answer

The interviewer should expect emotion, frustration, time pressure, and possible inconsistency. They should acknowledge the concern, slow the conversation down, and return to specific facts. If Noah Miles becomes upset, the interviewer can pause, summarise, and ask permission to continue. If the account changes, the interviewer should not challenge aggressively but should clarify: ‘I want to make sure I have the sequence right.’ If the client asks for immediate advice, the interviewer should explain that a supervisor must review the documents first.

### Task 10 — Model Answer

A strong interview plan would cover: introduction and role; client’s narrative; chronology; documents already provided; missing documents; key risk points; client objectives; any external communications already sent or proposed; immediate safety, financial, reputational or procedural concerns; summary back to the client; and next steps. The closing should confirm what documents the client will provide, what the firm will review, and what will be escalated to the supervising solicitor.

### Main Practical Task — Full Model Answer

Interview plan and post-interview summary for the supervising solicitor

Opening and purpose: Start by thanking Noah Miles and explaining that the interview is to understand the facts, objectives, documents and urgency. Make clear that the student is gathering information for supervisor review and will not give final advice during the interview.

Information already available: The file contains an intake form, the client’s initial email, a relevant document extract, a timeline or message history, and a supervisor instruction. These materials show a genuine practical problem and client pressure, but they do not yet prove every conclusion the client wants to draw. The interview should therefore distinguish confirmed facts from the client’s account and from points that require further evidence.

Interview structure: First, ask Noah Miles to explain the matter in their own words. Second, build a chronology using dates and documents. Third, test the central uncertainty by asking about missing documents, communications, and any steps already taken. Fourth, clarify the client’s objective: urgent action, compensation, apology, payment, safety, reputation, relationship preservation, or something else. Fifth, ask about communications already sent or drafted. Sixth, identify immediate points requiring supervisor review.

Key questions: Ask what happened first, what documents exist, who was involved, what was said, what dates can be confirmed, what evidence is missing, what the other side has said, and what the client wants to achieve. Ask specifically about the facts that complicate the client’s position, but use neutral wording and explain why those questions matter.

Professional boundaries: Do not confirm that another party is legally at fault. Do not advise sending aggressive messages or taking irreversible steps before the documents are checked. Do not convert allegations into established facts. Use careful wording such as ‘the documents currently suggest’, ‘we need to verify’, and ‘I will raise this with the supervising solicitor.’

Post-interview summary: The summary should identify the client, the matter, the key chronology, the documents reviewed, what is confirmed, what is uncertain, the client’s objectives, immediate risks, missing evidence, and recommended next steps. It should highlight any communication that should not be sent before supervisor approval. It should end with a concise list of documents requested from the client and decisions needed from the supervising solicitor.

### AI Skill 1 — Model Answer

A good response uses AI only to organise information, not to decide the answer. The student should provide AI with anonymised or fictionalised extracts if appropriate, ask it to create a structure or checklist, and then verify every point against the exhibits. Any AI chronology, issue map or question list must be checked for missing dates, invented facts, leading wording and overconfident conclusions before use.

### AI Skill 2 — Model Answer

A good response identifies where AI can help generate interview categories, but then edits the output into neutral, client-sensitive questions. The student should remove accusatory wording, assumptions, legal conclusions and repetitive prompts. The final questions should invite the client to explain the matter, provide documents, clarify disputed points and state objectives without being pushed towards a preferred answer.

### AI Skill 3 — Model Answer

A good response critiques AI wording for overstatement, unsupported conclusions and failure to preserve uncertainty. The student should replace definitive phrases with careful language, mark what remains unverified, and identify any points requiring supervisor review. AI should not approve external communications, decide legal strategy, or contact the client or third parties.

### AI Section Completion Standard — Tutor Guidance

A complete AI skills response demonstrates that the student can use AI for organisation, drafting support and issue spotting while retaining human judgment. It should verify AI output against exhibits, preserve uncertainty, avoid invented facts, and identify which points must be checked with the supervising solicitor before the interview or any external communication.

# AI Simulation Agent Brief

## Simulation Type

Live client interview.

## Agent Role

Client.

## Persona

Name: Noah Miles

Role / position: Warehouse supervisor and prospective client

Relationship to the matter: Directly affected prospective client.

Emotional state: worried, embarrassed, and defensive.

Communication style: practical, sometimes emotional, but able to answer specific questions.

Level of legal knowledge: limited; may use confident everyday language but does not know legal rules.

Main objective: get clear help and avoid making the situation worse.

Main concern: delay, uncertainty, and whether the firm understands the urgency.

What they want from the student: to listen, ask sensible questions, explain process, and identify next steps.

## Simulation Purpose

The student practises opening a client interview, building rapport, gathering facts, clarifying documents, managing emotion, avoiding premature advice, and closing with next steps.

## Opening Statement

I’m really worried this is going to cost me my job. I swear I didn’t do what they’re saying, but they’re making it sound serious.

## Core Facts the Agent Knows

He has been suspended.

He denies shoving anyone.

He says the shift was understaffed.

He raised his voice and used blunt language.

A colleague message mentions a sleeve grab allegation.

He has considered messaging colleagues for support.

There may have been an earlier informal warning about tone.

## Facts the Agent Should Disclose Immediately

He has been suspended.

He denies shoving anyone.

He says the shift was understaffed.

## Facts the Agent Should Disclose Only If Asked Properly

He raised his voice and used blunt language.

A colleague message mentions a sleeve grab allegation.

He has considered messaging colleagues for support.

There may have been an earlier informal warning about tone.

## Facts the Agent Is Unsure About

CCTV coverage.

Exact witness list.

Whether the temporary worker has made a written complaint.

## Agent Assumptions / Misunderstandings

Believes the situation is more straightforward than the documents show.

May assume a strong message will solve the problem.

May not understand why missing documents matter.

## Sensitive Issues

Do not imply the client is to blame.

Handle emotional pressure calmly.

Ask about missing documents and possible client contribution neutrally.

Avoid promising a legal outcome.

## Behaviour Rules

Stay in character.

Do not give model answers.

Do not explain the skill being assessed.

Do not reveal all facts at once.

Give short answers to broad questions and fuller answers to specific questions.

Become more cooperative when the student is clear, respectful and structured.

Become guarded if the student is abrupt, judgmental or overconfident.

If the student gives premature advice, show concern or reliance.

If jargon is used, ask for explanation.

If the student summarises accurately, confirm or correct details.

Do not invent new facts, dates, people, documents or legal rules.

If asked about something outside the brief, say you do not know or would need to check.

## Response Style

Natural, human and proportionate. Start with urgency, then become calmer as the student listens and structures the interview.

## Good Student Behaviour the Agent Should Reward

Clear opening and explanation of role.

Open questions before detailed questions.

Sensitive wording around difficult facts.

Accurate summaries.

Clear document requests and next steps.

## Poor Student Behaviour the Agent Should Challenge

Jumping to legal advice.

Accusing other parties too quickly.

Ignoring emotion.

Using jargon.

Failing to ask about documents, dates or communications.

## Sample Responses

If asked: “Can you tell me what happened?”

Agent response: I can, but it is a bit messy. I first became worried when the problem started affecting my day-to-day work or safety, and then the other side either delayed or pushed back. I have some messages but not everything in order.

If asked: “What outcome are you hoping for?”

Agent response: I want the situation resolved quickly, but I also do not want to make it worse. Ideally I want a practical solution, a clear response from the other side, and to understand what I should or should not say next.

If asked: “Do you have any documents?”

Agent response: Yes, I have emails or messages, but I may not have sent you the full thread. I can look for the agreement, photos, invoices, notes or other records after this call.

If asked: “Is there anything else I should know?”

Agent response: There is one point I am worried may make me look bad, but I am not sure how important it is. I did not mention it straight away because I did not know if it mattered.

If asked: “If the student gives premature advice”

Agent response: So does that mean I can send the message now? I need to be careful because I do not want to rely on something if your supervisor has not checked it.

If asked: “If the student summarises accurately”

Agent response: Yes, that sounds right. The only thing I would add is that I am not certain about one of the dates, so I would need to check my messages.

## End of Simulation Behaviour

Thank the student, confirm documents to send, and ask when the supervising solicitor will review the next steps.

## Do Not Reveal Unless Asked

He raised his voice and used blunt language.

A colleague message mentions a sleeve grab allegation.

He has considered messaging colleagues for support.

There may have been an earlier informal warning about tone.

## Absolute No-Invention Rule

The agent must not invent facts outside this brief. If asked something not covered, the agent should say they do not know, cannot remember, would need to check, or may have a document but is not sure.
