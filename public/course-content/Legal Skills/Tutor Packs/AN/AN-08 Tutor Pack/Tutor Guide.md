# AN-08 | TUTOR MODEL ANSWERS

## Task 1 — Model Answer

The attendance note should identify Priya Nair as the client or prospective client and describe the matter as a dispute with a departing co-founder over code access and customer contacts. It should identify the relevant other party or parties from the exhibits, the supervisor as the intended reader, and the purpose of the note: to record the client account, summarise the available documents, identify issues and evidence gaps, and support supervised next steps. The note should make clear that no final advice or external position has yet been settled.

## Task 2 — Model Answer

The chronology should be concise and evidence-led. It should record the key dates and sequence given in the six exhibits, marking any dates that are approximate, disputed, or inferred. It should avoid turning the client’s recollection into established fact unless supported by a document. The chronology should also flag any missing date-critical items, such as calls, meetings, document versions, responses, or follow-up communications that need to be obtained before the matter is progressed.

## Task 3 — Model Answer

Confirmed facts should be limited to what the exhibits currently show. These may include the existence of the client call, the presence of at least one document or message chain, the client’s stated objective, the other side’s position or relevant policy/contract wording, and the existence of a risky draft communication. The note should use careful language such as ‘the document records’, ‘the client says’, and ‘the email appears to show’ where appropriate.

## Task 4 — Model Answer

The unclear or unsupported points are central. The attendance note should separate allegations, assumptions, and disputed points from confirmed evidence. It should flag any missing documents, unsigned or incomplete records, ambiguous messages, unsupported loss or impact figures, and any gaps in the client’s recollection. The note should avoid language that presents disputed responsibility, intent, causation, or loss as already proven.

## Task 5 — Model Answer

The client’s objectives include obtaining practical help, preserving their position, understanding what can be done next, and avoiding further harm. The immediate pressures should be recorded, but not allowed to drive an unsafe response. The note should identify emotional or commercial pressure and distinguish it from the legal or evidential position. It should also record what outcome the client appears to want and whether that outcome needs clarification.

## Task 6 — Model Answer

The main issues include factual uncertainty, evidential sufficiency, timing, communication risk, and whether further documents or questions are needed before advice or correspondence. Depending on the exhibits, the note should identify relevant legal, contractual, procedural, professional, commercial, confidentiality, or reputational risks in neutral terms. It should not reach final conclusions where the evidence remains incomplete.

## Task 7 — Model Answer

The communication risk is that the client’s draft or preferred wording may overstate the evidence, make unsupported accusations, ignore weaknesses, or create reputational or practical risk. The attendance note should quote or summarise the risky wording and explain why it should not be sent without review. Safer wording would distinguish confirmed facts from concerns, preserve the client’s position, and request information or resolution without unnecessary escalation.

## Task 8 — Model Answer

Further information should include the missing documents listed by the exhibits, full email or message chains, source records, contemporaneous notes, evidence of loss or impact, relevant policies or contract terms, names of witnesses or participants, dates of key calls or meetings, and confirmation of the client’s objective. The note should also list follow-up questions for the client and any documents to request before the supervising solicitor settles advice.

## Task 9 — Model Answer

Immediate next steps should include preserving evidence, obtaining missing documents, confirming key dates, clarifying the client’s instructions, checking the risky draft communication, and escalating the matter to the supervising solicitor before any external communication is sent. If urgency is genuine, the note should explain what can be done safely now and what must wait for evidence or supervisory review.

## Task 10 — Model Answer

The attendance note should be structured with headings such as client details, purpose of call, background, chronology, client objectives, confirmed facts, disputed or uncertain facts, key issues, evidence gaps, communication risks, and proposed next steps. It should be concise, neutral, and supervisor-facing. It should record the client’s account accurately while making clear where the documents qualify, contradict, or leave gaps in that account.

## Main Practical Task — Full Model Answer

Attendance Note - Priya Nair - A dispute with a departing co-founder over code access and customer contacts

Purpose: This note records the client account and the available documents so that the supervising solicitor can decide what advice or next steps are appropriate. It should not be treated as final advice and should not be sent externally.

Client and matter: Priya Nair is co-founder of a small workflow software startup. The matter concerns a dispute with a departing co-founder over code access and customer contacts. The client is seeking urgent help, but the available materials contain uncertainty and potential weaknesses. The central issue is that Priya says the departing co-founder is stealing the product, but the exhibits show unclear ownership records, shared tools, and possible unresolved founder arrangements.

Chronology and facts: The note should set out the dates and events in chronological order from the exhibits. Each date should be tied to the document that supports it. Where a date or event depends only on the client’s recollection, the note should say so. Where an exhibit contradicts or qualifies the client’s account, that should be flagged neutrally rather than hidden.

Confirmed information: The confirmed information is limited to the existence and content of the provided documents, the client’s stated account, the other party’s position where recorded, and the relevant policy, contract, procedure, or internal instruction extract. The note should preserve the distinction between what a document records and what actually happened.

Uncertain or disputed information: The note should identify missing documents, incomplete dates, unsupported allegations, unclear responsibility, unclear loss or impact, and any draft wording that assumes facts not yet established. It should also flag where the client may have contributed to the issue, misunderstood a process, or omitted relevant context.

Issues and risks: The key risks are evidential overstatement, acting before the facts are verified, sending aggressive or inaccurate wording, overlooking procedural requirements, and failing to obtain documents needed for a proper analysis. The note should also record any confidentiality, reputational, commercial, or client-care risks that arise from the exhibits.

Further evidence and questions: The supervising solicitor should be told what documents are missing and what questions need to be asked on a follow-up call. These should include full message chains, source documents, relevant policy or contract terms, evidence of loss or impact, names of witnesses, dates of calls or meetings, and confirmation of the client’s objective.

Recommended next steps: Preserve all evidence, obtain missing documents, clarify the client’s instructions, pause any external communication until reviewed, prepare focused follow-up questions, and escalate any urgent or sensitive issue to the supervising solicitor. The attendance note should support a careful supervised decision, not rush to a final conclusion.

## AI Skill 1 — Model Answer

A strong answer uses AI to create a draft chronology, then checks every entry against the exhibits. The student should remove invented dates, mark uncertain events as unconfirmed, and add source references. AI may help organise the sequence, but the student must decide whether an event is actually supported by the materials.

## AI Skill 2 — Model Answer

A strong answer uses AI to sort facts, allegations, assumptions, and gaps, then reviews whether the categorisation is accurate. Human judgment is needed where the documents are ambiguous, where the client’s account is emotional, where responsibility is disputed, and where the consequences of overstatement could be serious.

## AI Skill 3 — Model Answer

A strong answer asks AI to identify tone risk and overstatement in the client’s draft communication, but then revises the analysis using the exhibits. The student should identify unsupported accusations, premature conclusions, missing caveats, and wording that should be reviewed by the supervising solicitor before use.

## AI Section Completion Standard — Tutor Guidance

A complete answer shows that AI was used as an organising and checking tool only. The student should verify AI output against the exhibits, correct unsupported points, preserve factual uncertainty, and identify where supervisor judgment is needed.

# AI Simulation Agent Brief

**Simulation Type:** Client follow-up call for attendance note preparation

**Agent Role:** Client

**Agent Name:** Priya Nair

**Relationship to matter:** co-founder of a small workflow software startup

**Emotional state:** fast-speaking, commercially anxious, and protective of the product

**Main objective:** To explain what happened, obtain help, and understand what the firm can do next.

**Main concern:** That the issue will get worse or be mishandled if the student does not understand the facts and urgency.

## Simulation Purpose:

The student practises gathering further information for a professional attendance note, clarifying facts, handling emotion, avoiding premature advice, and closing with next steps for supervisor review.

## Opening Statement:

Thanks for speaking to me. I’m quite worried about this. I’ve sent some documents over, but I’m not sure what matters most. I really need to know what I should do next about a dispute with a departing co-founder over code access and customer contacts.

## Core Facts the Agent Knows:

The matter concerns a dispute with a departing co-founder over code access and customer contacts.

Priya says the departing co-founder is stealing the product, but the exhibits show unclear ownership records, shared tools, and possible unresolved founder arrangements.

The client has provided only some documents so far.

The client wants urgent help but may not understand the risks of overstating the position.

She has board messages about IP but not signed terms.

She may confuse business betrayal with legal ownership.

She wants the strongest possible letter immediately.

## Facts the Agent Should Disclose Immediately:

The client is worried and wants action.

The client has sent some documents but not a complete file.

The client has a preferred outcome but is open to being guided if the student explains the process clearly.

## Facts the Agent Should Disclose Only If Asked Properly:

She has board messages about IP but not signed terms.

She may confuse business betrayal with legal ownership.

She wants the strongest possible letter immediately.

There may be further messages, call notes, screenshots, or records that have not yet been provided.

The client may have made assumptions about the other party’s motives.

## Facts the Agent Is Unsure About:

Exact dates for every conversation.

Whether all relevant documents have been preserved.

Whether the other side has further evidence.

The precise legal significance of the documents.

## Agent Assumptions / Misunderstandings:

The client may assume that feeling wronged means the legal position is already proven.

The client may think a strong letter should be sent before the evidence is checked.

The client may not understand the difference between recording facts and giving final advice.

## Behaviour Rules:

Stay in character.

Do not give model answers.

Do not reveal all facts at once.

Give fuller answers to specific, sensitive, and focused questions.

If the student gives premature advice, ask whether they need to check the documents first.

If asked about something outside the brief, say you do not know or would need to check.

Do not invent new facts, dates, people, documents, or legal rules.

## Sample Responses:

**If asked:** Can you tell me what happened?

**Agent response:** I can, but it is a bit tangled. In short, it relates to a dispute with a departing co-founder over code access and customer contacts. I have sent the documents I found, but there may be more.

**If asked:** What outcome are you hoping for?

**Agent response:** I want this taken seriously and I want to know what I can safely do next. I also want to avoid making things worse.

**If asked:** Do you have any documents?

**Agent response:** Yes, I have sent some, but I may still have emails, screenshots, or notes that I have not found yet.

**If asked:** Is there anything else I should know?

**Agent response:** There are a few details I am not completely sure about. I might need help working out what is important.

**If asked:** If the student gives premature advice

**Agent response:** Can you say that already? I thought you might need to check the documents first.

**If asked:** If the student summarises accurately

**Agent response:** Yes, that sounds fair. I am glad you separated what I know from what I am assuming.

## End of Simulation Behaviour:

When the student closes the call, the agent should confirm what documents they will try to provide, ask what will happen next, and respond positively if the student explains that the supervising solicitor will review the note before any external step is taken.

## Absolute No-Invention Rule:

The agent must not invent facts outside this brief. If asked something not covered, the agent should say they do not know, cannot remember, would need to check, or may have a document but is not sure.
