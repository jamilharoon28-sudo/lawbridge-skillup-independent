# LW-10 | TUTOR MODEL ANSWERS

## Task 1 - Model Answer

The audience is the consumer client. The purpose is to produce a written explanation and next steps note that helps the reader understand the position, practical risk, and next step. The response should be plain-English, structured, realistic, and careful and should not go beyond the evidence.

## Task 2 - Model Answer

Key facts include: Daniel financed a used van on 14 March; 14 March purchase and finance agreement; The finance agreement names the finance provider and refers customer complaints about vehicle quality to the dealer first, with escalation to the finance provider if unresolved. The facts should be presented in a logical order and tied back to the main written response rather than listed without analysis.

## Task 3 - Model Answer

The issues are factual uncertainty, communication risk, evidence gaps, and the need to write in a way that is useful without overstating the legal position. The student should identify both the supported concerns and the limits of the evidence.

## Task 4 - Model Answer

The client objective is to write a plain-English explanation of the position, evidence gaps, and next steps. The written response should advance that objective while preserving credibility, avoiding unnecessary escalation, and leaving room for supervisor review.

## Task 5 - Model Answer

The communication must explain the supported facts, the practical significance of those facts, the points that remain uncertain, and the recommended next steps. It should be specific enough to be useful and simple enough for the intended reader.

## Task 6 - Model Answer

The student should avoid unsupported allegations, definitive conclusions not proved by the exhibits, unnecessary admissions, threats that have not been approved, and language that may damage credibility. In this scenario, avoid: telling Daniel to stop payments immediately; stating the van was definitely faulty at sale without expert evidence; threatening social media allegations; ignoring the dealer’s repair attempts.

## Task 7 - Model Answer

Important caveats include the lack of complete evidence, unresolved factual disputes, and the need for supervisor review before making stronger assertions. The communication should distinguish known facts from allegations, assumptions, and points requiring follow-up.

## Task 8 - Model Answer

A sensible structure is: short introduction, confirmed factual summary, key issue or risk, caveats/missing evidence, practical recommendation, and next steps. The tone should be plain-English, structured, realistic, and careful.

## Task 9 - Model Answer

Key points should include: what faults are evidenced and when they arose; what the finance provider and dealer have said; why independent inspection evidence matters; what next step should be taken before stronger demands. These should be drafted in complete, audience-appropriate sentences rather than as internal notes.

## Task 10 - Model Answer

The final communication should be accurate, concise, and usable. It should address the reader directly, include only supported facts, make uncertainty clear, and end with an actionable next step.

# Main Practical Task - Full Model Answer

Dear Daniel,

Thank you for sending the purchase timeline, finance extract, dealer emails, and mechanic text. The documents show a genuine ongoing problem with the van, but there are still some evidence gaps we should deal with before sending a stronger complaint.

The timeline is helpful. You bought the van on finance on 14 March. Warning lights appeared by 2 April, the dealer reset a sensor on 5 April, limp mode returned on 19 April, a valve was replaced on 25 April, and the warning light returned on 9 May. This supports that the issue has recurred despite repair attempts.

The dealer says it believes the van was roadworthy at sale and wants another diagnostic appointment. The mechanic text suggests a possible pre-existing EGR issue, but it is not a formal inspection report. It should be treated as a lead, not final proof.

I would not recommend stopping finance payments or making public allegations without advice from the supervising solicitor. That may create separate risks and could weaken the focus of the complaint.

The next step is to write to the finance provider and dealer with a clear chronology, ask for their proposed resolution, and request that any further inspection is recorded in writing. You should also consider obtaining an independent diagnostic report. Once we have that report, the firm can advise on the strength of a more formal position.

Kind regards,
[Name]

## AI Skill 1 - Model Answer

A good student may use AI to propose a structure, but should revise it so that it matches the audience, the factual record, and the purpose of the communication. The student should check that the AI has not introduced new facts or legal conclusions.

## AI Skill 2 - Model Answer

A good student should identify and remove overstatement, blame language, premature threats, unsupported admissions, and vague drafting. AI suggestions should be treated as drafting options, not final judgment.

## AI Skill 3 - Model Answer

A good student should compare the AI fact/assumption list against all six exhibits. Confirmed facts should be separated from disputed points, inferred points, and missing evidence. Any hallucinated or unsupported AI output should be corrected.

## Behaviour Rules

Stay in character and do not give model answers.

Ask the student to justify wording by reference to the exhibits.

Push back if the student uses unsupported allegations or promises.

Reward clear, measured, audience-aware communication.

Do not invent new facts beyond the exhibits and brief.
