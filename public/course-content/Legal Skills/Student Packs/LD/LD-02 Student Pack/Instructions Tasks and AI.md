## How to Use This Simulation


● Read the exhibits as if this is a live matter file.

● Do not rely on the exhibit index as a summary of the issues.

● Extract the relevant clauses, dates, facts, figures, obligations, deadlines and missing information yourself.

● Record where each point appears, using exhibit labels and clause numbers where available.

● Separate what is known from what is assumed, disputed or missing.

● Prepare a practical output for a supervisor or client using only the evidence in the file.


## Learning Outcomes


● Draft clauses dealing with AI outputs, human review and responsibility.

● Address confidentiality and third-party rights risk.

● Use defined terms consistently.

● Explain legal uncertainty without relying on jurisdiction-specific conclusions.

● Prepare alternative drafting options for negotiation.


## Student Task Brief


A business wants to use AI tools to create marketing materials, reports and software-related content. You must draft contract wording that allocates risk and responsibility for AI-assisted outputs.


## Student Practice Tasks


Complete the following practice tasks as a trainee-style matter file review. Your answers must cite the relevant exhibit, clause, date, email, log entry or policy wording. Do not give broad commentary where a specific document reference is available.

## Detailed Practice Tasks


01 Identify what the client wants the clause to achieve.

02 Define AI tool, AI output and input materials.

03 Draft ownership or usage rights wording cautiously.

04 Draft human review and approval obligations.

05 Draft confidentiality and input restrictions.

06 Draft third-party claim cooperation wording.

07 Draft record-keeping obligations for prompts and outputs.

08 Prepare a supplier-friendly fallback.

09 Prepare a client-friendly fallback.

10 Write a drafting rationale note.


## Working Method


● Start with the exhibit index, but do not treat it as a summary of the answer.

● Mark every clause, date, figure and disputed factual point that affects advice or drafting.

● Separate confirmed facts, disputed facts, assumptions, missing information and legal points requiring further checking.

● Keep supervisor-facing outputs concise: lead with the practical risk, then the evidence, then the next step.

• Use only the fictional scenario materials and separated exhibits. Do not add real client facts or confidential information.

• Treat AI as an assistant, not an authority, lawyer, tutor or decision-maker.

• Check AI statements against the exhibits and any source that needs legal verification.

• Do not let polished wording hide uncertainty, missing facts or supervisor approval points.
